Brisa de Año Ranch

By Lizette Calderon & Raffaella Cerruti  |  June 9, 2020

"Everyone is in need of nutritionally rich produce.
It shouldn’t be considered a luxury good."

“Brisa de Año Farm is a small organic farm located in Pescadero, California, along the Pacific coast line. Owned and operated by three friends — Cole Mazariegos, Veronica Mazariegos, and Cristóbal Cruz Hernandez — this farm is known for its variety of fruits, vegetables and flowers.

Brisa de Año is also known for their specialty crops, such as garbanzo and pinquito beans, which are difficult to grow on the coast. They also cultivate specialty peppers like chile de agua, chilacayote, a species of squash original to Oaxaca, and ratte potatoes, rare in California and originally from France. Their produce can be found at local restaurants and small grocery stores in Half Moon Bay, Good Eggs in SF, and at the Burlingame Farmers Market on Sundays.

Brisa de Año plans to farm an additional two acres this year where they will plant even more varieties. They also want to focus on retaining and refining the health of the soil and finding more sustainable ways to farm using less water.

Their long term vision is to provide avenues for small scale farmers to thrive and to help these farmers with their farming practices to corroborate the continuation of farming. The average age of retirement is 58 years old for farmers in the U.S. so it's important to pass down this knowledge to the new generational farmers.

In addition to providing the platform for new upcoming farmers they also want to figure out ways to have produce be equitably viable for themselves, while also priced fairly for the consumer. They do not want to exclude anyone, so they are strategizing to create the best model to approach this very important issue. “Everyone is in need of nutritionally rich produce, and it shouldn’t be considered a luxury good,” explains co-owner Cole Mazariegos.

The coronavirus pandemic has brought a lot of uncertainty and unpredictability to the farm, causing a huge loss in revenue and forcing them to rethink their long-term vision.

These challenges have forced Brisa de Año to quickly pivot to focusing on consumer sales, since so many of the restaurants they count on as customers have dramatically reduced their weekly orders.

Thanks to the Farm Fresh Food Relief program Brisa de Año has had an excellent outlet for their produce to make up for some of the lost sales. “We’ve really appreciated being a part of [the Farm Fresh Food Relief] program,” Cole Mazariegos explains. “We’re happy that our produce can go to families that need it during this time of societal need.”
